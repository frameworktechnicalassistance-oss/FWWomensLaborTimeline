Women, Labor & Exploitation in the U.S.

A timeline of how policies and systems shaped women's labor protections and vulnerabilities.

1911 (Context)

Triangle Shirtwaist Factory Fire

Triangle Shirtwaist Factory fire, New York City. 146 workers—mostly immigrant women—were killed after being trapped inside locked factory floors. :contentReference[oaicite:0]{index=0}

What happened: Factory fire killed 146 workers, most young immigrant women.

Vulnerability: Locked doors prevented escape—a common labor control practice.

“Workers died because safety and control were prioritized over their lives.”

1935–1938

New Deal Labor Laws

What happened: Minimum wage, overtime, and collective bargaining established.

Vulnerability: Domestic and agricultural workers excluded—disproportionately Black women and immigrant women.

1995

El Monte Thai Garment Slavery Case

Site of the El Monte garment compound where Thai women were held in forced labor conditions behind barbed wire. Workers reported being confined and paid extremely low wages. :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}

What happened: 72 Thai women held in captivity and forced to work in a garment factory.

Vulnerability: Debt bondage, immigration coercion, isolation.

“Conditions described as modern-day slavery in the United States.”

2000

Trafficking Victims Protection Act

What happened: First federal law defining labor trafficking.

Limitation: Relief tied to law enforcement cooperation.

2015

Nail Salon Investigation

What happened: Major exposé revealed widespread wage theft and unsafe conditions.

Vulnerability: Debt, immigration status, and piece-rate pay.

2022

Operation Blooming Onion

What happened: Large forced labor case in agriculture.

Vulnerability: Employer-controlled housing and coercion.
